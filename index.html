About Me

I am a junior attending school at Franklin W. Olin College located in Needham, MA. I am majoring in General Engineering with a focus in Engineering Design, but am a Mechanical Engineer at heart. I was born and raised in Houston, Texas and am fifth of seven children. My passions include photography, design, ultimate frisbee, music, and Texas.

Hopper

Design Nature Project

For this project, I was tasked with designing and building a bio-inspired hopper using only a small set of materials. This set did not contain any traditional fasteners, and students were challenged with using joints and fitted spring steel to create our hoppers. A delay between the setting of the hopper and the hop was required, and was another area where I was challenged to come up with creative solutions to produce a delayed trigger.

  • Spring 2014

Disc Quality Detector

Real World Measurements Project

At the end of our Real World Measurements class, my group and I were tasked with creating some device that measured something. Almost all of us being on the Ultimate Frisbee team, we decided to create a device that could measure both scratching and warping of the disc. Using a strain guage and a design resembling a record player, we traced over the surface of the disc, using two separate paths of filtering that allowed us to capture both small scratches on the surface of the disc as well as bending occuring over the entirety of the disc.

  • Spring 2014

Underactuated Hand

Mechanical Prototyping Project

For the second half of the Mechanical Prototyping course, myself along with three other students were tasked with designing and fabricating three separate iterations of an underactuated hand, refining our design along the way. The first iteration was required to be made entirely out of plywood and sheet metal. The second iteration was required to be entirely 3D printed, with polymers being the only exception. For the final iteration, we were able to use any of the previous materials. The project culminated in a final competition, where each team's hand was tasked with picking up things of various sizes, shapes, and weights. Our hand performed very well. To see what the final iteration looked like and read more about our final design, visit our team's project site.

  • Spring 2014

Principles of Engineering Classroom Redesign

Summer Research Opportunity

Over the summer, another student and I got the opportunity to spearhead a complete classroom redesign for the Principles of Engineering classroom, which was known for the mess that the class had accrued over the years. We stripped the class bare, and began from scratch, making the room the most it could be. We took a very user-oriented approach to the project, conducting interviews and creating needs of the space before ideation began. We then came up with over 500 ideas, 50+ addressing each need. We jumped right into sketching and prototyping, using large amounts of cardboard to prototype at a 1:1 scale. Durable prototypes were then created using a 4'x8' Shopbot that we recieved, built, and learned how to use over the ten weeks of the project. After user testing of the durable prototypes, deployable prototypes were created and deployed for this semester's course.

The large standing desks in the center of the room was my individual project over the summer, designed based on a Opendesk design. The table is assembled completely without fasteners, and the surfaces are easily replaceable after extended use. Power strips are found easily accessible along the center of the table, and foam tiles relief the pain of standing for extended periods of time. We are continuing to iterate on our design, and our project has sparked more space redesigns around campus. We had a Raspberry Pi set up taking photos of the room every three minutes and uploading it to flickr. At the end of the summer, we were able to create an amazing time lapse video. Follow the link to also see the thousands of photos that were captured during those ten weeks.

  • Summer 2014

Laud - Mobile App Design

User-Oriented Collaborative Design Final Project

In User-Oriented Collaborative Design, my team of five students were looking in depth at the needs of our user group, Boston-area subway musical buskers. We went through the first three portions of the design process, conducting user research through interviews with buskers. We then formed several personas based off of the performers we interacted with. From these personas, we formed several user needs and used these needs to drive rapid idea generation. After narrowing down our many concepts, we arrived at Laud.

Laud is a mobile app concept that gives commuters an avenue to learn more about the street performers that they see every day on the T and gives them an opportunity to provide payment in whatever form the busker prefers. We found that not all performers were looking to earn money, but instead were looking for interactions with riders of the T or social media attention. We created an app that transmitted the performer's wants to the many commuters listening to their music, and gives them the opportunity to provide that payment.
